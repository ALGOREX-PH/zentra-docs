The proof is produced by `payment_policy.circom` — a Circom 2.2.3 circuit on the
**bn128 / BN254** curve, with a depth-4 recipient Merkle tree.

## Public inputs

Fourteen signals are public, in this exact order. The same ordering is shared by
the circuit, the contract, and the serialization codec.

<PublicInputsTable />

## Private inputs

These never leave the prover:

- `privateMaxAmount`, `privateDailyLimit` — the policy thresholds
- `policySalt` — the secret that hides the commitment
- `pathElements[4]`, `pathIndices[4]` — the Merkle proof for the recipient
- `invoicePreimage` — hashed to `invoiceHash`
- `nonce` — entropy for the nullifier

## The six constraint families

1. **Commitment opening** — `Poseidon(privateMaxAmount, privateDailyLimit, recipientRoot, assetId, policySalt) === policyCommitment`.
2. **Merkle membership** — `Poseidon(recipient)` is a leaf under `recipientRoot` (depth 4, boolean path indices).
3. **Range checks** — `amount <= privateMaxAmount` and `prevSpent + amount <= privateDailyLimit`, each bit-bounded with `Num2Bits(64)`.
4. **State transition** — `newSpent === prevSpent + amount` and `newActionCount === prevActionCount + 1`.
5. **Invoice binding** — `Poseidon(invoicePreimage) === invoiceHash`.
6. **Nullifier derivation** — `Poseidon(agentAddress, policyCommitment, contractAddress, nonce) === nullifier`.

<Callout title="Why bit-width bounds matter">
Without the `Num2Bits(64)` guards, a malicious prover could exploit field
wraparound to make `amount <= max` appear true. Bounding every comparand closes
that hole.
</Callout>
